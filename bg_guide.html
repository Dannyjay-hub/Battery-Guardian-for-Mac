Battery Guardian Guide

Understanding Forensic Diagnostics & Health Grading (v{{VERSION}})

Battery Guardian was built to uncover the hidden truth about your MacBook's battery. While macOS provides a simple "Normal" or "Service Recommended" label, Battery Guardian goes deeper by analyzing the raw hardware data from the TI Smart Battery System gas gauge chip using physics-based forensic checks.

Why is this necessary? The secondary market for MacBooks is flooded with devices carrying "fake" or "reprogrammed" batteries. Dishonest sellers use hardware tools (like JCID or NLBA1) to flash the firmware on aging batteries, resetting the cycle count to zero and forcing health to 100%, to sell worn-out Macs at a premium. Battery Guardian catches these operations using physics that cannot be faked without creating internal contradictions.

1. The Health Score (/100)

The large number in the center ring is your Forensic Health Score. All batteries start at 100.

  • 100 to 90 (Genuine & Healthy): All forensic checks pass and the battery retains strong capacity.
  • 89 to 70 (Aging): Hardware checks passed, the battery is genuine but has experienced natural chemical aging. Deductions scale with how much capacity has been lost.
  • 69 to 40 (Suspicious or Worn Out): The battery may be heavily degraded, triggering minor flags, or contain uncalibrated third-party cells without active spoofing.
  • Below 40 (Spoofed / Forged): At least one forensic check detected a physical impossibility. The firmware has been actively tampered with.

2. Core Hardware Metrics

The six metric cards show raw values pulled directly from the TI chip via ioreg. These are unsmoothed, unaveraged hardware readings.

Cycles

A full discharge and recharge counts as one cycle. A genuine battery legally ages, reprogrammers reset this to zero.

Health

The precise ratio of current maximum capacity to design capacity, parsed directly from AppleRawMaxCapacity, not the software-smoothed value shown in System Settings.

Entropy

The capacity gap between the strongest and weakest cell. Real cells always diverge. A gap of exactly 0 mAh after 5+ cycles is physically impossible, values were hard-coded.

Battery Made

Manufacture date derived from TotalOperatingTime, the chip's cumulative hour counter since first activation. Provides a legitimacy signal independent of cycle count.

Write Ratio

DataFlashWriteCount ÷ CycleCount. Shows how intensively the chip's memory has been written relative to cycle history. A useful secondary indicator for chip activity.

Temp

Live reading from the internal thermistor. Third-party batteries sometimes wire resistors instead of real thermistors, temperatures will be static or physically implausible.

3. The 8 Forensic Checks

Battery Guardian runs 8 independent checks on every scan. All 8 appear in the log, either as a pass, fail, or informational notice. Each check is grounded in TI Smart Battery System firmware specifications.

Fail Trigger: 40 pts

Physics Check: Zero Entropy

What it is: The TI Impedance Track™ algorithm maintains an independent Qmax value per cell, continuously refined during calibration. Lithium cells made in separate production runs always diverge in chemistry. After 5+ cycles, genuine cells always have measurable Qmax variance.

Why it fails on fakes: When firmware is flashed with fake capacity data, programmers copy a single value across all three cell registers. If all Qmax values are identical after 5+ cycles, the data is hard-coded, not measured.

Fail Trigger: 25 pts

Internal Resistance Gap

What it is: Qmax is the battery's chemical storage capacity. FCC (Full Charge Capacity) is the usable energy delivered under load. As a battery ages, internal resistance builds up and energy is lost to heat, FCC falls progressively below Qmax.

Why it fails on fakes: After 30+ cycles, a gap of less than 1% between Qmax and FCC is physically impossible. Both values likely share the same hardcoded source. Raw mAh values are compared to avoid integer-rounding artifacts.

Fail Trigger: 30 pts

Firmware Hack: Lazy Cloning

What it is: After 5+ calibration cycles, Qmax[0] should be naturally refined below DesignCapacity. An exact match is the most common spoof pattern, scammers press "Copy Design to Qmax" in their flashing software.

Why it fails on fakes: No genuine battery retains factory-exact capacity after several cycles. The TI algorithm always refines it downward.

Fail Trigger: 30 pts

DOD0 Calibration Tampering

What it is: DOD0 records the Depth of Discharge from the most recent calibration run. A genuine calibration always discharges slightly less than DesignCapacity, no real cell ever delivers exactly its rated spec.

Why it fails on fakes: If DOD0 equals DesignCapacity exactly, the calibration history was fabricated. This catches spoofed batteries that patch Qmax but forget to sanitise the calibration subsystem.

Fail Trigger: 50 pts

Clock Integrity

What it is: The chip samples temperature every ~225 seconds (TemperatureSamples) and also maintains a cumulative hour counter (TotalOperatingTime). Both measure elapsed time through completely independent mechanisms. On genuine hardware they agree within ~5%.

Why it fails on fakes: A spoofer who resets TotalOperatingTime to hide the battery's age rarely knows to also reset TemperatureSamples. A discrepancy ≥20% exposes the tampering. A single genuine MacBook measured 0.005% discrepancy.

Fail Trigger: 50 pts

Calibration Timeline Paradox

What it is: CycleCountLastQmax records the cycle number at which the most recent Qmax calibration ran. It can never legally exceed CycleCount, a calibration cannot happen in the future.

Why it fails on fakes: If a spoofer resets the cycle counter after a calibration event, the chip's own records will show a calibration that hasn't happened yet. This is a binary, deterministic signal: either physically valid or mathematically impossible.

Fail Trigger: 30 pts

Chip Origin

What it is: MaximumPackVoltage is the highest pack voltage ever recorded in the chip's lifetime. The TI Cell Under Voltage (CUV) floor for a 3-cell MacBook pack is 3,000 mV × 3 = 9,000 mV minimum.

Why it fails on fakes: A MaximumPackVoltage below 9,000 mV means this chip has never operated inside a 3-cell stack, it was taken from a 2-cell device (phone or tablet) and re-programmed to impersonate a MacBook chip.

Fail Trigger: 40 pts

Frozen Clock

What it is: TotalOperatingTime is a cumulative hour counter that increments continuously whenever the system is powered on. Requires two scans taken 30+ hours apart to compare.

Why it fails on fakes: If the counter hasn't changed between two scans taken 30+ hours apart, the firmware timer is disabled or spoofed. The 30-hour threshold (above the chip's confirmed 24-hour update cycle) eliminates false positives from Macs that were off between scans.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: My score is 70 but the verdict is still "Genuine". Is my battery okay?
Yes. A score of 70 means you have an old, worn battery that has lost some charge capacity. It is genuinely aged, not forged.

Q: How often should I scan?
Once is enough to get a verdict. The Automation tab lets you schedule daily silent scans, useful for tracking degradation over time or building a history log before selling a laptop.

Q: Will Battery Guardian drain my battery?
No. Reading the IORegistry via ioreg is a zero-impact system call, equivalent to reading a text file. The entire scan completes in under a second and uses no measurable battery power.

Q: How is Battery Made calculated?
It is derived from TotalOperatingTime, the chip's internal hour counter since first activation. Subtracting those hours from today gives the approximate manufacture date. This is the same technique used by other battery tools.

Q: Can a battery pass all checks and still be fake?
In practice, no. Consumer battery flashing tools (JCID, NLBA1, and similar) can only overwrite a limited set of DataFlash registers, typically cycle count, design capacity, and Qmax. The majority of Battery Guardian's checks examine registers that the TI chip updates automatically via hardware: TotalOperatingTime increments on its own every cycle, TemperatureSamples updates on every temperature measurement, and lifetime protection registers like MaximumPackVoltage are recorded by the chip's hardware safety layer. You cannot simply "set" these to fake values, even if written, the hardware immediately resumes updating them from that point. Freezing them requires disabling the TI firmware counters at a level that goes far beyond DataFlash writes, and attempting it risks triggering the chip's Permanent Failure latch, which irreversibly bricks it. The Frozen Clock check exists specifically to catch the most common evasion: a frozen TotalOperatingTime.
